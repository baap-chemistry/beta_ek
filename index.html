Rate of Reaction

The rate of a chemical reaction is the change in concentration of reactants or products per unit time. It indicates how fast a reaction occurs.

Average Rate (ravg): Measured over a finite time interval Δt. It represents the overall speed over a duration:
ravg = -Δ[A]/Δt = +Δ[P]/Δt

Instantaneous Rate (rinst): The rate of reaction at a specific instant of time. Expressed using differentials:
rinst = -d[A]/dt = +d[P]/dt

CBSE Formula (Reactant to Product: aA → bB) Rate = -1/a * d[A]/dt = +1/b * d[B]/dt

Order vs. Molecularity

Students frequently confuse these two key kinetic terms. Let's compare them side-by-side to understand why they are drastically different.

Reaction Order is an experimental quantity determined by adding exponents of concentration terms in the Rate Law equation. It can be fractional or zero.

Molecularity is a theoretical concept representing the number of reacting species (atoms, ions, or molecules) that must collide simultaneously to bring about the reaction. It can only be an integer (1, 2, or 3).

Rate Law Expression Rate = k[A]x[B]y  ⇒  Overall Order (n) = x + y
Property Order Molecularity
Definition Sum of exponents in Rate Law Number of colliding molecules
Nature Experimental value Theoretical value
Values Can be 0, fraction, or integer Only integers (1, 2, 3)
Application Applies to elementary & complex Applies to elementary only

Integrated Rate Equations

CBSE Class XII focuses specifically on the integrated rate laws of Zero-Order and First-Order reactions. These allow us to calculate concentrations at any given time.

Zero-Order: Rate of reaction is independent of the reactant concentration.
Equation: [A]t = -kt + [A]0
Half-life (t1/2): Directly proportional to initial concentration.

First-Order: Rate of reaction is proportional to the concentration of reactant.
Equation: ln[A]t = -kt + ln[A]0  ⇒  k = 2.303/t * log([A]0/[A]t)
Half-life (t1/2): Completely independent of initial concentration.

Zero Order

k = ([A]0 - [A]t)/t

t1/2 = [A]0 / (2k)

First Order

k = 2.303/t * log([A]0/[A]t)

t1/2 = 0.693 / k

Collision Theory & Temperature Effect

Why do reactions speed up dramatically at higher temperatures? Why do we need catalysts?

Collision Theory: For a reaction to occur, reactant molecules must collide. However, only effective collisions form products. An effective collision requires:
• Energy Barrier: Molecules must possess a minimum energy called Threshold Energy (Activation Energy, Ea + Average Energy).
• Orientation Barrier: Molecules must collide in the correct spatial alignment.

Arrhenius Equation: Quantifies how rate constant (k) depends on temperature: k = A * e-Ea/RT. A increase of 10 K in temperature generally doubles the rate constant due to a doubling of effective collisions.

Arrhenius Equation k = A • e-Ea/RT
A = Frequency Factor
Ea = Activation Energy (J/mol)
R = Gas Constant (8.314 J/K/mol)
T = Temperature in Kelvin

Kinetics in the Real World

Fun & Fact Learn

Discover how chemical reaction rates control everyday physical processes, from biological clockworks to environmental conservation.

Arrhenius in Nature
🦗

Firefly Flashing Frequencies

Did you know that fireflies flash faster on warm summer nights? The chemical reaction responsible for their bioluminescence (luciferin oxidation catalyzed by luciferase) follows the Arrhenius Equation. The rate of the biochemical reaction increases exponentially with temperature, prompting faster flashes!

Domestic Chemistry
❄️

The Freezer Kinetic Barrier

We freeze food to prevent spoilage—but why? Food spoilage is a set of multi-step biochemical reactions. Reducing the temperature from 25°C (room temp) to -18°C (freezer) severely reduces the average kinetic energy of the reactants. This means only a tiny fraction of molecules can cross the activation energy barrier, slowing the decomposition rate to a near standstill!

Heterogeneous Catalysis
🚗

Catalytic Converter Matchmakers

Automobile exhaust containing poisonous Carbon Monoxide (CO) and Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) passes through a catalytic converter lined with Platinum, Palladium, and Rhodium. The metal surfaces act as catalysts by providing a solid surface for adsorption, aligning reactants perfectly, and lowering the activation energy to speed up conversion to harmless CO2 and N2.

Nuclear Kinetics
⏳

Carbon Dating & First-Order Decay

All radioactive decay processes follow strict First-Order Kinetics. The half-life of Carbon-14 (14C) is a constant 5,730 years, completely independent of how much carbon is present. By measuring the remaining concentration of 14C in ancient wood or bones, archaeologists can integrate first-order decay equations to date them precisely.

Visual Kinetics
🧪

The Landolt Iodine Clock

The Iodine Clock reaction is a famous experiment where a solution suddenly turns dark blue/indigo in an instant. This occurs because of a series of competing reactions with different rates. The first reaction consumes iodine slowly, and once a critical reactant runs out, a rapid second reaction takes over, changing the color. It's a gorgeous visual representation of consecutive kinetics!
